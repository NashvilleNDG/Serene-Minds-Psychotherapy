What Is Grief Therapy?

Grief therapy is a specialized form of psychotherapy that provides a structured, compassionate space to process loss — in all its complexity, at your own pace, without judgment. It goes beyond the sympathy of friends and family to offer professional support that understands the full psychological, emotional, and even physical dimensions of grief.

Grief is not simply sadness. It is a profound reorganization of the self in response to loss — an adjustment to a world that no longer contains something or someone who was central to it. It can involve anger, guilt, relief, numbness, anxiety, physical exhaustion, and profound disorientation, sometimes all within the same hour. Without support, grief can become stuck — turning into complicated grief, depression, or chronic loneliness that significantly affects quality of life.

Grief therapy does not rush the process or try to eliminate grief. It honors the grief as the natural, necessary response to love and loss that it is — while helping you find your footing, process the full range of what you are feeling, and gradually reconnect with life and meaning. At Serene Minds Psychotherapy, all sessions are conducted via secure, HIPAA-compliant online video — available to anyone in Florida from the comfort and privacy of their own home.

Types of Grief and Loss We Help With

Loss takes many forms — and all of them are valid reasons to seek support. Fram works with individuals navigating every type of grief experience, always with an approach that honors the uniqueness of each person's loss and each person's way of grieving.

💔 Death of a Loved One

The death of a parent, partner, child, sibling, or close friend is one of the most profound losses a person can experience. Grief therapy provides a safe, unhurried space to mourn the person you have lost, process the full range of emotions their death has generated, and gradually find a way to carry them with you as you rebuild your life without them physically present.

👶 Pregnancy Loss and Infertility

The loss of a pregnancy — through miscarriage, stillbirth, or termination — and the grief of infertility are among the most isolating and misunderstood forms of loss. Many people suffer in silence, feeling that their grief is not legitimate because the loss was not of a person others knew. Therapy provides a space where this grief is fully honored, without minimization and without comparison.

🐾 Pet Loss

The grief of losing a beloved pet is real, significant, and frequently dismissed by those who have not experienced it. For many people, a pet is their closest companion — their daily routine, their source of comfort, their reason to come home. Fram provides a judgment-free space where pet loss grief is taken seriously and supported with the same compassion as any other profound loss.

💑 Relationship Loss

The end of a significant relationship — whether through divorce, separation, or the gradual dissolution of a friendship — involves genuine grief that is often unacknowledged. There are no rituals for this kind of loss, no bereavement leave, no community of mourners. Therapy provides the space to grieve the relationship, the shared future, and the version of yourself that existed within it.

🏥 Anticipatory Grief

Anticipatory grief — the grief that begins before a loss has occurred, when a loved one is seriously ill or dying — is one of the most exhausting and least recognized forms of grief. It involves mourning someone who is still present while simultaneously preparing for their absence. Therapy helps individuals navigate this complicated emotional terrain with greater awareness and self-compassion.

🌍 Ambiguous Loss

Ambiguous loss occurs when someone is physically present but psychologically absent — as in dementia, addiction, or severe mental illness — or when someone is physically absent but psychologically present — as in estrangement, disappearance, or immigration. This type of loss is particularly difficult because it lacks the clarity and social recognition of death. Therapy helps make sense of what can feel like unresolvable grief.

😔 Complicated Grief

Complicated grief — also called prolonged grief disorder — occurs when grief becomes stuck rather than moving through its natural course. Symptoms include persistent intense longing for the person who died, difficulty accepting the loss, bitterness or anger that does not diminish, and an inability to engage with life months or years after the loss. Therapy specifically addresses the factors keeping grief complicated and helps it move toward integration.

🌱 Loss of Identity or Life as Known

Not all grief is about the death of a person. The loss of a career, a health diagnosis that changes everything, the end of a chapter of life, children leaving home, or the gradual loss of physical ability all involve genuine grief — a mourning of a self or a life that no longer exists. Therapy honors these losses and helps individuals find meaning and identity in what comes next.

🌺 Cultural and Diasporic Grief

For South Asian and immigrant communities, grief is often shaped by cultural traditions, family expectations, and the complexity of mourning while living far from home. Fram offers grief therapy in English, Gujarati, and Hindi — providing culturally sensitive support that honors the specific ways grief is experienced and expressed within different cultural contexts.

How Fram Supports Grief: Evidence-Based Therapy Approaches

CBT adapted for grief helps individuals identify and address the thought patterns that are keeping grief complicated or preventing integration. These include thoughts like “I should be over this by now,” “grieving means forgetting them,” “if I stop crying I am betraying them,” or “I will never be happy again.” CBT for grief also addresses the avoidance behaviors that are common in grief — avoiding places, people, or activities associated with the person who died — and gradually helps the individual re-engage with life without feeling they are abandoning their grief or the person they have lost.

Narrative therapy helps grieving individuals construct a coherent story of their loss — making meaning of what happened, honoring the relationship they had, and finding a way to maintain a continuing bond with the person they have lost while also moving forward. It helps individuals separate who they are from their grief — recognizing that they are more than their loss — and begin writing the next chapter of their life with intention and with the person they have lost woven into it rather than left behind.

Grief often generates intense resistance — a desperate wish for things to be different, a fighting against reality that exhausts and prolongs suffering. Mindfulness-based grief therapy helps individuals develop the capacity to be present with their grief without being overwhelmed by it and without fighting against it — approaching each wave of emotion with compassion and openness rather than fear or resistance. Over time this mindful presence with grief paradoxically reduces its intensity and allows it to move more freely.

Meaning-making approaches to grief — developed from the work of researchers like Robert Neimeyer — help individuals find meaning in their loss experience rather than simply recovering from it. This does not mean finding a reason why the loss happened. It means finding meaning in how you respond to it — what the relationship meant, what it taught you, how it has changed you, and how the person or thing you have lost can continue to be part of your identity and your life going forward. For many people this approach is profoundly transformative.

Traditional models of grief suggested that the goal was to “let go” of the person who died and move on. Research now consistently shows that healthy grief involves maintaining a continuing bond with the person who has died — keeping them present in your life in a transformed way — rather than severing the relationship. Continuing bonds therapy helps individuals find ways to honor, remember, and maintain their relationship with who they have lost while also re-engaging with life and new attachments.

Grief can produce profound ambivalence about healing — a part of the person that wants to feel better and a part that fears that feeling better means moving on, forgetting, or betraying the person who has died. Motivational Interviewing addresses this ambivalence directly and compassionately — exploring the person's own values, their relationship with the person they have lost, and their genuine reasons for wanting to live fully again. It builds internal motivation for healing without ever minimizing the grief. Fram is a certified MI practitioner.

Signs That Grief Therapy May Help You

  • ✓ You have experienced the death of a loved one and are struggling to cope
  • ✓ Your grief feels stuck — not moving or evolving despite time passing
  • ✓ You are experiencing persistent depression, anxiety, or exhaustion since your loss
  • ✓ You feel unable to accept that the loss has happened
  • ✓ You feel intense guilt, anger, or regret related to your loss
  • ✓ You have lost interest in life and cannot imagine finding meaning again
  • ✓ You are avoiding everything that reminds you of the person or thing you have lost
  • ✓ You feel that nobody around you truly understands what you are going through
  • ✓ Your relationships, work, or daily functioning have been significantly affected
  • ✓ You are experiencing anticipatory grief as a loved one approaches death
  • ✓ You have experienced a loss that others do not recognize as legitimate
  • ✓ You want support in finding a way to honor your loss while also moving forward
