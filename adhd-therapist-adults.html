What Is Adult ADHD?

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder is a neurodevelopmental condition — present from birth, rooted in how the brain regulates attention, executive function, impulse control, and emotional response. It is not a childhood condition that people grow out of. Research shows that approximately 60% of children with ADHD continue to meet diagnostic criteria in adulthood — and many adults are diagnosed for the first time in their 30s, 40s, or beyond, after years of struggling without explanation.

Adult ADHD presents differently from the hyperactive child most people picture. In adults, hyperactivity often manifests as internal restlessness — difficulty with stillness, boredom intolerance, a constant need for stimulation — rather than the visible physical overactivity of childhood. The attentional difficulties, executive function deficits, and emotional dysregulation remain central — often more impairing in the demands of adult life than they were in childhood.

ADHD is significantly underdiagnosed in women, in people from ethnic minority backgrounds, and in high-achieving individuals who have developed sophisticated compensatory strategies that mask the underlying difficulties — until the demands of adult life finally exceed what those strategies can manage.

What ADHD Therapy for Adults Helps With

Adult ADHD affects every area of life. Fram works with adults navigating the full range of ADHD-related challenges — always with an approach that honors the genuine strengths of the ADHD brain alongside its difficulties.

🎯 Focus and Attention

The hallmark difficulty of ADHD — sustaining attention on tasks that are routine, unstimulating, or demand prolonged effort — affects work performance, academic achievement, and daily functioning. Therapy develops practical strategies for managing attention that work with the ADHD brain rather than against it.

📋 Executive Function and Organization

Planning, prioritizing, initiating tasks, managing time, and following through on commitments — the executive function skills that most people take for granted — are consistently difficult for adults with ADHD. Therapy provides structured, concrete strategies for building these skills in ways that accommodate the ADHD brain's specific strengths and limitations.

⏰ Time Management and Procrastination

Time blindness — the inability to accurately perceive and manage time — is one of the most impairing features of adult ADHD. Combined with the difficulty initiating tasks that ADHD creates, procrastination becomes a chronic pattern that generates real professional and personal consequences. Therapy addresses both the practical strategies and the psychological weight that chronic procrastination carries.

💔 Relationships and Communication

Forgotten commitments, impulsive responses, difficulty listening, and emotional dysregulation all create significant relationship challenges for adults with ADHD. Therapy develops the interpersonal skills and self-awareness that support healthier, more consistent, and more satisfying relationships.

😤 Emotional Dysregulation

Emotional dysregulation — particularly rejection sensitive dysphoria, intense emotional reactions, and low frustration tolerance — is one of the most significant and most underrecognized features of adult ADHD. Therapy develops the emotion regulation skills that reduce reactivity and improve both self-regulation and relational functioning.

😔 ADHD and Depression

Adults with ADHD experience depression at significantly higher rates than the general population — as a direct consequence of years of underachievement, relationship difficulties, and the accumulated psychological burden of living with an unrecognized condition. Therapy addresses both the ADHD and the depression as an integrated clinical picture. See our dedicated depression and anxiety therapist page.

😰 ADHD and Anxiety

Anxiety is the most common co-occurring condition in adult ADHD — developing partly as a neurobiological feature of ADHD and partly as a psychological response to the chronic stress of managing ADHD demands. Integrated therapy addresses both conditions simultaneously.

🌍 ADHD in South Asian and Immigrant Adults

For South Asian and immigrant adults with ADHD — where the condition may have been completely unrecognized within a cultural context that attributes difficulties to laziness or lack of effort, and where help-seeking carries significant stigma — finding a therapist who understands both the clinical and cultural dimensions is particularly valuable. Fram offers therapy in English, Gujarati, and Hindi.

💼 Career and Professional Impact

Adult ADHD has a significant and consistent impact on career functioning — affecting performance, relationships with colleagues and managers, and the ability to sustain employment in environments that do not accommodate neurodivergent working styles. Therapy helps adults with ADHD identify their genuine strengths, develop workplace strategies, and advocate for the adjustments that support their best performance.

How Fram Works With Adults With ADHD: Evidence-Based Approaches

CBT adapted for adult ADHD is the most extensively researched psychotherapy for the condition — and it addresses both the practical challenges and the psychological consequences of living with ADHD. CBT-ADHD targets the negative self-beliefs that ADHD generates — “I am lazy,” “I cannot be trusted,” “I will always fail” — alongside the specific executive function challenges through structured, skills-based behavioral work. Multiple randomized controlled trials have found CBT-ADHD significantly reduces ADHD symptoms and improves functioning in adults.

Adults with ADHD frequently experience profound ambivalence about change — a pattern of starting things with genuine enthusiasm that fades before completion, of knowing what would help and being unable to consistently implement it. MI addresses this directly — exploring the person's own values, goals, and reasons for wanting things to be different, building genuine internal motivation rather than adding external pressure to an already overloaded system. Fram is a certified MI practitioner with over 20 years of clinical experience.

DBT's four skill sets — mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness — directly address the emotional dysregulation that is central to adult ADHD. For adults whose ADHD is significantly complicated by emotional reactivity, rejection sensitivity, and relationship difficulties, DBT skills training provides a structured framework for developing the self-regulation capacities that ADHD impairs.

Mindfulness — adapted for the ADHD brain — trains the attentional system directly, developing the capacity for non-judgmental, sustained attention that ADHD disrupts. Short, varied, movement-integrated mindfulness practices are most effective for adults with ADHD — building attentional capacity over time in a way that complements rather than conflicts with ADHD neurology.

A core component of adult ADHD therapy is psychoeducation — understanding what ADHD actually is, how it manifests in your specific presentation, and — critically — what the genuine strengths of the ADHD brain are. The creativity, the hyperfocus capacity, the ability to thrive under pressure, the divergent thinking — these are real and significant. Therapy helps adults with ADHD understand and leverage their strengths rather than only managing their difficulties.

Unlike pure coaching, therapy for adult ADHD addresses both the practical executive function challenges and the emotional and psychological dimensions of living with ADHD. Practical strategies — time management systems, task initiation techniques, organization structures — are integrated into a therapeutic framework that also addresses the self-worth, relationships, and mood consequences of the condition.

Signs That ADHD Therapy May Help You

  • ✓ You consistently struggle to sustain attention on tasks — particularly routine or administrative ones
  • ✓ Procrastination is a chronic pattern that creates real professional or personal consequences
  • ✓ You frequently lose track of time, underestimate how long things take, or miss deadlines
  • ✓ You start things with enthusiasm but rarely finish them
  • ✓ Emotional reactions feel intense and disproportionate — particularly to criticism or rejection
  • ✓ You have been told you have potential you are not living up to
  • ✓ Relationships are affected by forgotten commitments, impulsive responses, or difficulty listening
  • ✓ You have suspected ADHD for years but never pursued assessment or support
  • ✓ You have an ADHD diagnosis but have never received therapy specifically designed for it
  • ✓ Depression or anxiety has developed alongside ADHD-related difficulties
  • ✓ You feel shame or self-criticism about patterns you have been unable to change through willpower alone
  • ✓ You function significantly better under pressure, urgency, or high interest than in routine demands
Note: Therapy for ADHD works best alongside accurate diagnosis. If you suspect ADHD but have not yet been assessed, Fram can discuss the assessment pathway with you during the free consultation and provide therapeutic support alongside or prior to formal diagnosis.
