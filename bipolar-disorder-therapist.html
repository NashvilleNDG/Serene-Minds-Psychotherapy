What Is Bipolar Disorder — and What Does It Actually Feel Like?

Bipolar disorder is a mood disorder characterized by episodes of depression alternating with episodes of mania or hypomania — periods of elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with significantly increased energy, reduced need for sleep, and altered judgment and behavior. It affects approximately 2.8% of the adult population — making it one of the most common serious mental health conditions worldwide.

Bipolar disorder exists on a spectrum. Bipolar I involves full manic episodes that typically require hospitalization and cause significant disruption to functioning. Bipolar II involves hypomanic episodes — less severe than full mania, not requiring hospitalization — alternating with major depressive episodes. Cyclothymia involves chronic, fluctuating mood instability that does not reach the threshold of full manic or depressive episodes but significantly affects daily life.

Despite its prevalence and treatability, bipolar disorder is significantly misunderstood — both by the general public and frequently by the people who have it, many of whom are misdiagnosed with unipolar depression for years before the full picture emerges. The depressive phase is where most of the burden lies — people with bipolar disorder spend significantly more time in depression than in mania or hypomania — and it is frequently undertreated.

What bipolar disorder actually feels like from the inside is less dramatic than its portrayal suggests and more exhausting. The vigilance of monitoring your own mood. The fear of the next episode. The grief of what episodes have cost — relationships, careers, opportunities. The medication side effects. The stigma. And the specific psychological work of building a stable life inside a condition that makes stability genuinely challenging.

How Fram Works With Bipolar Disorder: Evidence-Based Approaches

CBT adapted specifically for bipolar disorder addresses the condition at multiple levels simultaneously. It targets the negative automatic thoughts that deepen depressive episodes and the grandiose, threat-minimizing thoughts that sustain hypomanic and manic escalation. It addresses the behavioral patterns — sleep disruption, increased activity, reduced help-seeking — that trigger and sustain episodes. And it builds the specific cognitive and behavioral skills — thought monitoring, activity scheduling, sleep regulation — that directly support mood stability. Multiple randomized controlled trials confirm CBT-BD significantly reduces depressive symptoms, reduces relapse rates, and improves psychosocial functioning compared to medication alone.

IPSRT is one of the most evidence-based psychotherapies specifically developed for bipolar disorder. Built on the finding that disruptions in social rhythms — daily patterns of sleep, activity, social stimulation, and routine — are among the most powerful triggers for mood episodes, IPSRT helps individuals stabilize these rhythms as a direct mood stabilization strategy. It also addresses the interpersonal difficulties that bipolar disorder creates — grief of the lost healthy self, role transitions after diagnosis, and relationship difficulties — through an interpersonal therapy framework.

Structured psychoeducation — understanding bipolar disorder, its causes, its triggers, its treatment, and its management — is one of the most consistently evidence-based interventions in the bipolar disorder literature. Group psychoeducation programs for bipolar disorder have been shown in multiple randomized controlled trials to significantly reduce relapse rates and hospitalizations. At the individual level, psychoeducation helps people understand their specific pattern of the condition and develop personalized mood management strategies. See our psychotherapy for bipolar disorder page.

DBT is particularly valuable for people with bipolar disorder who experience significant emotional dysregulation — intense, rapidly shifting emotional states that exceed the mood episode pattern itself. Its four core skill sets — mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness — directly address the emotional instability and impulsivity that bipolar disorder can generate, providing practical tools for daily functioning.

MBCT has shown growing evidence of benefit for bipolar disorder — particularly for reducing depressive relapse and for developing the metacognitive awareness that allows mood changes to be observed and responded to before they escalate. The capacity to notice early mood shifts with curiosity rather than alarm is one of the most clinically important skills in bipolar disorder management.

People with bipolar disorder frequently experience profound ambivalence about treatment — particularly about medication adherence and lifestyle changes that feel restrictive. The hypomanic state in particular can generate resistance to treatment — because it feels good and because accepting treatment means accepting the diagnosis fully. MI addresses this ambivalence with genuine respect, building authentic motivation for the management strategies that produce stability. Fram is a certified MI practitioner.

Signs That a Bipolar Disorder Therapist May Help You

  • ✓ You have been diagnosed with bipolar disorder and have not received specialist therapy alongside medication
  • ✓ You experience episodes of depression alternating with periods of elevated mood, energy, or reduced need for sleep
  • ✓ You have been treated for depression that has not fully responded — and wonder whether bipolar disorder may be a better explanation
  • ✓ Medication alone has not produced the stability and quality of life you want
  • ✓ You struggle with medication adherence and want to address the barriers honestly
  • ✓ Episodes are significantly affecting your relationships, career, or daily functioning
  • ✓ You live in fear of the next episode and want to develop better early warning and prevention skills
  • ✓ Anxiety is a significant feature alongside your bipolar disorder
  • ✓ The identity and self-worth consequences of bipolar disorder are as burdensome as the episodes themselves
  • ✓ You want to understand your specific pattern of the condition rather than just responding to episodes
  • ✓ You are managing the condition alone and finding that isolation is making it harder
  • ✓ You want to build genuine long-term stability rather than simply surviving between episodes
Important: If you are experiencing a manic episode with severely impaired judgment or safety concerns, or thoughts of suicide or self-harm, please call 988, call 911, or go to your nearest emergency room immediately.
