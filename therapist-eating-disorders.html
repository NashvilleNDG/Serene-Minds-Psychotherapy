What Are Eating Disorders — and What Drives Them?

Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions characterized by persistent disturbances in eating behavior and a preoccupation with food, weight, and body image that significantly impairs physical health, psychological wellbeing, and daily functioning. They are not lifestyle choices, attention-seeking behaviors, or phases. They are complex conditions with significant neurobiological, psychological, and social components — and they require proper clinical treatment.

The most common eating disorders include Anorexia Nervosa — characterized by severe food restriction, intense fear of weight gain, and a distorted perception of body size. Bulimia Nervosa — characterized by cycles of binge eating followed by purging behaviors — vomiting, laxative use, excessive exercise — driven by guilt and shame. Binge Eating Disorder — characterized by recurrent episodes of eating large quantities of food rapidly, in distress, and without compensatory behaviors — the most common eating disorder in the general population. Avoidant Restrictive Food Intake Disorder — ARFID — characterized by extremely limited food intake based on sensory or aversive features rather than body image concerns. And Other Specified Feeding and Eating Disorders — OSFED — which include presentations that do not fully meet the criteria for the above but cause equivalent distress and impairment.

What drives eating disorders — beneath the specific behaviors — is almost always a combination of intolerable emotional experience, a self-worth that has become entirely body-dependent, and the use of food and control as a coping mechanism that works in the short term and destroys in the long term. Effective therapy addresses these underlying drivers rather than only the eating behaviors — because behavior change without psychological change does not produce lasting recovery.

What Eating Disorder Therapy at Serene Minds Helps With

Eating disorders affect every dimension of life. Fram works with individuals navigating all eating disorder presentations — with an approach that honors the complexity of recovery without judgment.

🍽 Restrictive Eating and Anorexia

The profound fear of weight gain, the distorted body image, and the severe restriction that characterize anorexia require a specific therapeutic approach that addresses both the psychological drivers and the cognitive distortions that sustain restriction. Therapy works alongside medical and nutritional support to address the psychological component of recovery.

🔄 Binge-Purge Cycles and Bulimia

The shame-driven cycle of bingeing and purging — where the temporary relief of the binge and the compensatory purge maintain the eating disorder while deepening the shame that drives it — responds well to structured CBT that interrupts the cycle at multiple points simultaneously.

🧠 Binge Eating Disorder

Binge Eating Disorder — the most common eating disorder — involves recurrent episodes of eating large quantities of food rapidly and in distress, without compensatory behaviors. It is significantly underdiagnosed and undertreated. Therapy addresses both the binge behaviors and the emotional dysregulation and shame that drive them.

🪞 Body Image and Self-Worth

The distorted and cruel relationship with the body — and the self-worth that has become entirely conditional on body size, shape, and eating behavior — is central to eating disorder recovery. Therapy directly addresses body image distortion and the development of a self-worth that is genuinely unconditional.

😰 Emotions and Eating

Eating disorders develop because food and control provide a way to manage emotions that feel otherwise unmanageable — anxiety, shame, emptiness, loneliness, anger. Therapy develops the emotional regulation skills that make food-based coping unnecessary — addressing the feelings that the eating disorder has been managing.

😔 Depression and Eating Disorders

Depression co-occurs with eating disorders at very high rates — both as a driver and as a consequence. Integrated therapy that addresses both conditions simultaneously produces significantly better outcomes than treating either in isolation. See our psychotherapy for depression page.

😰 Anxiety and Eating Disorders

Anxiety — particularly around food, eating situations, body image, and social judgment — is one of the most consistent features of eating disorders across all presentations. Therapy addresses the anxiety directly alongside the eating disorder behaviors it sustains. See our psychotherapy for anxiety page.

🌍 Cultural Dimensions of Eating Disorders

In many South Asian and immigrant communities, eating disorders are significantly underrecognized — partly because the Western stereotype of eating disorders does not match the cultural context, and partly because body image pressures in South Asian communities can look different from those in mainstream Western culture. 🌱 Recovery and Relapse Prevention

Eating disorder recovery is not linear — and relapse is a common part of the recovery journey rather than evidence of failure. Therapy builds the relapse prevention skills, the emotional resources, and the self-compassion that support sustained recovery over time.

How Fram Works With Eating Disorders: Evidence-Based Approaches

CBT-Enhanced — developed specifically for eating disorders by Christopher Fairburn — is the most extensively researched and most widely recommended psychological treatment for bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, and OSFED. It addresses the specific maintaining mechanisms of eating disorders at multiple levels simultaneously — the dietary restraint that triggers bingeing, the overvaluation of shape and weight that drives restriction and purging, the perfectionism and low self-esteem that underlie the condition, and the interpersonal difficulties that sustain it. Multiple randomized controlled trials confirm CBT-E produces significant, lasting improvement in the majority of people with bulimia and binge eating disorder who complete treatment.

DBT was originally developed for borderline personality disorder but has become one of the most evidence-based treatments for eating disorders — particularly those driven by emotional dysregulation and impulsivity. Its four core skill sets — mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness — directly address the emotional experience that eating disorder behaviors are managing, providing alternative coping tools that make food-based regulation less necessary.

ACT is particularly valuable for eating disorders because it directly addresses the psychological inflexibility — the struggle to tolerate difficult thoughts, feelings, and bodily sensations — that sustains eating disorder behaviors. By developing psychological flexibility and values-based action, ACT helps individuals relate to difficult internal experiences without needing to control or escape them through eating disorder behaviors.

Addressing body image distortion is a specific and essential component of eating disorder therapy — not an add-on. Fram integrates evidence-based body image interventions — including cognitive restructuring of appearance-related beliefs, mirror exposure where appropriate, and the development of a body relationship based on function and compassion rather than appearance and control.

Eating disorders frequently develop in the context of trauma — including childhood abuse, sexual trauma, and the cumulative trauma of chronic emotional invalidation. Trauma-informed therapy ensures that the pace and approach of eating disorder treatment respects the trauma history that often underlies it — addressing the trauma alongside the eating disorder rather than treating the behaviors in isolation from their origins.

Ambivalence about recovery is one of the defining features of eating disorders — particularly anorexia, where the condition is often experienced as an identity and a coping mechanism simultaneously. MI addresses this ambivalence with genuine respect and without pressure — exploring both the function the eating disorder serves and the genuine desire for a different life, building authentic motivation for change from within. Fram is a certified MI practitioner.

Signs That Eating Disorder Therapy May Help You

  • ✓ Your relationship with food, eating, or your body is causing significant distress or interference with daily life
  • ✓ You restrict food intake significantly or follow rigid rules about eating that feel impossible to break
  • ✓ You experience recurrent episodes of eating large amounts of food rapidly in distress
  • ✓ You use purging behaviors — vomiting, laxatives, excessive exercise — to compensate for eating
  • ✓ Your self-worth is significantly or entirely dependent on your body size, shape, or what you ate
  • ✓ You experience intense anxiety, shame, or guilt around food and eating situations
  • ✓ You avoid social situations involving food or eating with others
  • ✓ You have a distorted perception of your body size that others consistently contradict
  • ✓ Eating disorder behaviors are affecting your physical health, relationships, or daily functioning
  • ✓ You have attempted to change your relationship with food alone and found the patterns keep returning
  • ✓ Depression or anxiety is significantly intertwined with your eating disorder
  • ✓ You are ready to address what is underneath the eating disorder behaviors — not just the behaviors themselves
Important: Eating disorders can have serious medical consequences. If you are experiencing medical symptoms related to an eating disorder — including heart palpitations, fainting, severe weakness, or signs of malnutrition — please seek medical attention immediately. If you are experiencing thoughts of suicide or self-harm please call or text 988. For eating disorder support please contact the National Alliance for Eating Disorders helpline at 1-866-662-1235.
