What Is Codependency — and How Do You Know If You Have It?

Codependency — originally identified in the context of relationships with people struggling with addiction but now understood as a much broader relational pattern — describes a specific dynamic in which one person's sense of identity, worth, and emotional regulation becomes excessively dependent on another person's needs, feelings, and approval. It involves a systematic neglect of one's own needs in favor of an excessive focus on managing, fixing, rescuing, or pleasing others.

Codependency typically develops in families where a child's role was to manage the emotional needs of a parent — through illness, addiction, mental health difficulties, or simply emotional immaturity — or where love was consistently conditional on compliance, performance, or emotional self-suppression. The child learns that their own needs are secondary, that conflict is dangerous, that being needed is the only secure basis for love, and that the self must be subordinated to maintain connection. These patterns do not stay in the family of origin — they travel into every significant adult relationship.

The result in adult life is a specific relational dynamic — excessive caretaking, extreme difficulty saying no, an inability to tolerate others' distress without attempting to fix it, profound difficulty identifying genuine personal needs and feelings, a self-worth that is entirely contingent on being needed and approved of, and the specific exhaustion of living entirely in service of others while one's own inner life goes unacknowledged. Codependency is not weakness or excessive kindness — it is a deeply embedded relational survival strategy that therapy can genuinely change.

“Codependency is not about loving too much. It is about knowing yourself too little — and therapy is where you find your way back.”

What Codependency Therapy at Serene Minds Helps With

Codependency affects every dimension of relational life. Fram works with individuals navigating all aspects of codependent patterns — in romantic relationships, family systems, friendships, and professional contexts.

🔄 Excessive Caretaking and Rescuing

The compulsive need to fix, rescue, and manage others — at the expense of your own needs, energy, and wellbeing — is the most visible feature of codependency. Therapy addresses the anxiety and self-worth beliefs that drive compulsive caretaking and develops the capacity for genuine care that includes appropriate limits.

🚫 Difficulty Setting Limits

The inability to say no — to requests, to unreasonable demands, to behavior that crosses your own values — without overwhelming anxiety and guilt is central to codependency. 🪞 Lost Identity and Self-Neglect

The codependent person has typically spent so long focused on others that they genuinely do not know what they feel, need, want, or value — because these things have never been prioritized or developed. Therapy provides the space to discover and develop a genuine, stable self that exists independently of relationships.

💔 Relationships With Difficult or Dependent Partners

Codependency frequently draws people toward partners who are struggling — with addiction, mental health difficulties, narcissism, or emotional immaturity — because the familiar dynamic of being needed provides a sense of purpose and identity. Therapy addresses both the codependent pattern and its expression in current relationships.

😰 Anxiety Around Others' Emotions

Codependent people typically experience profound anxiety when those they care about are distressed — because they have learned that others' emotional states are their responsibility to manage. Therapy develops the capacity to tolerate others' distress without being compelled to fix it — one of the most liberating shifts codependency therapy produces.

😔 Resentment and Emotional Depletion

The inevitable consequence of giving everything to others while neglecting the self — resentment, exhaustion, and a quiet fury at having sacrificed so much — is addressed in therapy through understanding its origins and developing the assertiveness that prevents its accumulation.

🔗 Codependency and Attachment

Codependency is deeply rooted in attachment — typically anxious attachment where love felt conditional on performance, compliance, or managing the caregiver's emotional world. Therapy addresses the attachment roots of codependency alongside its current relational expression. See our attachment therapist page.

🌍 Cultural Dimensions of Codependency

In many South Asian and immigrant families, the values that create codependency — self-sacrifice, collective prioritization, deference to family need — are deeply culturally embedded rather than simply personal patterns. How Fram Works With Codependency: Evidence-Based Approaches

CBT addresses the specific beliefs that sustain codependency — “my needs are less important than others',” “if I stop caring for them something terrible will happen,” “I am only valuable when I am needed,” “saying no means I don't love them.” These beliefs feel absolutely true to codependent people — they were formed in early environments where they were functionally accurate — but they are not accurate reflections of current reality. CBT identifies these core beliefs, examines their origins and their current validity, and develops more balanced and self-inclusive beliefs that support healthier relational functioning.

Since codependency has its deepest roots in anxious attachment — where love was conditional on performance, compliance, or emotional caretaking — attachment-focused therapy addresses these origins directly. Understanding how early attachment shaped the current relational pattern — and developing earned secure attachment through the consistent, boundaried therapeutic relationship — produces fundamental shifts in how the person relates to intimacy, need, and self-worth.

Schema therapy targets the early maladaptive schemas that codependency creates and sustains — particularly self-sacrifice, subjugation, and approval-seeking schemas that drive the automatic prioritization of others at the expense of self. Working with these deeply embedded patterns at the level where they actually operate — below conscious reasoning — produces the lasting change that more surface-level interventions cannot achieve alone.

DBT's interpersonal effectiveness skills are central to codependency recovery — providing structured, practical frameworks for asking for what you need, setting limits while maintaining relationships, and navigating the specific relational situations that codependency makes most difficult. The DEAR MAN, GIVE, and FAST skill sets address the assertiveness deficits that codependency creates with concrete, learnable tools.

ACT is particularly valuable for codependency because it directly addresses values clarification — helping people identify what genuinely matters to them, independent of what others need or expect. Living in alignment with authentic values rather than others' needs is both the goal of codependency therapy and the mechanism through which it produces lasting change. ACT also addresses the experiential avoidance that sustains codependency — the way avoiding the anxiety of disappointing others keeps the pattern locked in place.

Codependent people typically feel profound ambivalence about changing — because the role of caretaker and rescuer, however exhausting, also provides identity, purpose, and the only kind of love they learned to trust. MI explores this ambivalence honestly — understanding both what codependency costs and what it provides — and builds genuine internal motivation for a different way of relating. Fram is a certified MI practitioner.

Signs That Codependency Therapy May Help You

  • ✓ You consistently prioritize others' needs above your own — to a degree that leaves your own needs unmet
  • ✓ You find it nearly impossible to say no without overwhelming guilt or anxiety
  • ✓ Your sense of worth is significantly dependent on being needed or approved of
  • ✓ You feel responsible for managing others' emotions — and anxious when you cannot fix their distress
  • ✓ You have difficulty identifying what you genuinely feel, need, or want
  • ✓ You are consistently attracted to people who are struggling and in need of your help
  • ✓ You give and give until resentment builds — then start giving again rather than addressing the pattern
  • ✓ Setting limits in relationships produces overwhelming anxiety, guilt, or fear of abandonment
  • ✓ You lose yourself in relationships — adapting so completely to others that you cannot find yourself
  • ✓ You were raised in a family where your role was to manage a parent's emotional world
  • ✓ You feel guilty when you prioritize your own needs — as though self-care is somehow selfish
  • ✓ You are exhausted by the relentless demands of caring for everyone except yourself
Note: Codependency exists on a spectrum. Many people recognize some of these patterns without identifying fully with codependency as a label. Therapy addresses the specific patterns that are causing distress — whatever the degree.
