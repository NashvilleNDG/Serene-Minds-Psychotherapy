What Is Postpartum Depression?

Postpartum depression — also called postnatal depression — is a clinical depressive episode that develops in the weeks or months following childbirth. It is distinct from the baby blues — the brief period of tearfulness, emotional sensitivity, and mood fluctuation that affects up to 80% of new mothers in the first two weeks after birth and typically resolves without treatment. Postpartum depression is more persistent, more severe, and significantly more impairing — and it requires professional support.

Postpartum depression can develop at any point in the first year after birth — not only in the immediate postpartum period. Many mothers experience their first symptoms at two to three months, when the initial adrenaline of new parenthood fades and the reality of chronic sleep deprivation, identity change, and relentless demand sets in. Some develop significant symptoms as late as six to twelve months postpartum.

The causes of postpartum depression are multiple and interacting — hormonal changes following delivery, sleep deprivation, the identity transition of new motherhood, relationship changes, social isolation, previous history of depression or anxiety, and the specific pressures and expectations that surround new motherhood in many families and communities. Understanding these causes is part of what therapy provides.

Postpartum depression is treatable — and the earlier treatment begins, the faster and more complete the recovery. At Serene Minds, all sessions are via secure, HIPAA-compliant online video — available from home, fitting into the reality of new parenthood rather than requiring you to rearrange it.

Becoming a mother involves one of the most significant identity transitions a person can experience — and postpartum depression frequently makes this transition feel like loss rather than growth. How Fram Treats Postpartum Depression: Evidence-Based Approaches

CBT is the most extensively researched psychotherapy for postpartum depression — and it translates particularly well to the postpartum context because of its practical, skills-based nature. CBT addresses the negative automatic thoughts that postpartum depression generates — “I am a bad mother,” “I am failing my baby,” “I should be feeling this differently,” “something is permanently wrong with me” — alongside the behavioral withdrawal and avoidance that deepen depression in the postpartum period. Multiple randomized controlled trials confirm CBT's effectiveness for postpartum depression, including online delivery. See our dedicated postpartum depression therapy resource for more information.

IPT is specifically recommended for postpartum depression in international clinical guidelines — because of its direct focus on the role transitions, grief, and interpersonal difficulties that new motherhood involves. IPT helps mothers process the identity change of new parenthood, navigate relationship changes with partners and family, address grief related to the loss of previous life structures, and build stronger interpersonal support. Research consistently shows IPT to be highly effective for postpartum depression.

MBCT is particularly valuable for postpartum depression because of its capacity to interrupt the rumination and self-critical thinking that sustains it — teaching mothers to relate to difficult thoughts and feelings with curiosity rather than being overwhelmed by them. MBCT is also specifically suited to the postpartum context because its mindfulness practices develop the present-moment awareness that supports genuine engagement with the baby.

Behavioral activation — gradually and systematically re-engaging with meaningful activities alongside the demands of new parenthood — directly addresses the withdrawal and reduced engagement that sustains postpartum depression. The challenge of behavioral activation in the postpartum period is identifying activities that are genuinely restorative within the constraints of new parenthood — and Fram's approach adapts this evidence-based technique specifically to the postpartum context.

One of the most immediately healing interventions for postpartum depression is thorough psychoeducation — understanding what postpartum depression is, why it develops, and what is actually happening psychologically and physiologically. For mothers who have been suffering in silence and shame, having their experience accurately named and clinically validated frequently produces immediate and significant relief alongside the beginning of genuine recovery.

Many mothers with postpartum depression feel profound ambivalence about seeking help — guilt about taking time for themselves, uncertainty about whether what they are experiencing warrants professional support, and the exhaustion that makes initiating anything feel impossible. MI meets mothers exactly where they are — building genuine internal motivation for engaging with treatment without adding pressure to an already overloaded system. Signs That Postpartum Depression Therapy May Help You

  • ✓ You have felt persistently sad, empty, or numb since giving birth — lasting more than two weeks
  • ✓ You are experiencing difficulty feeling connected to or bonded with your baby
  • ✓ You feel overwhelmed by new parenthood to a degree that feels beyond what others seem to experience
  • ✓ You are experiencing significant anxiety — including intrusive thoughts about harm to your baby
  • ✓ You feel like you have lost yourself and are not sure how to find your way back
  • ✓ You feel guilty about not feeling the joy you expected motherhood to bring
  • ✓ Your relationship with your partner has become significantly strained since the birth
  • ✓ You are struggling with thoughts that your baby would be better off without you
  • ✓ The exhaustion you feel goes beyond what sleep deprivation alone explains
  • ✓ You had depression or anxiety before or during pregnancy and it has worsened since birth
  • ✓ You are not coping as well as you expected or hoped — and the gap is frightening
  • ✓ You have been struggling in silence because you feel you should be coping better
Important: If you are experiencing thoughts of harming yourself or your baby, please seek immediate help — call 988, call 911, or go to your nearest emergency room immediately. Postpartum mental health crises are medical emergencies and require immediate professional support.
